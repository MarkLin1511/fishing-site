Local guide

Yes, you really can fish in Los Angeles.

The LA River is not one giant free-for-all. The smart move is to use legal public access, stay aware of safety and water quality, and learn which nearby waters are easiest for beginners.

See the spots Open hotspots map

License rule

California requires a sport fishing license for most anglers age 16 and older in inland waters.

LA River access rule

The MRCA recreation zones allow public fishing in designated areas, typically from Memorial Day to the end of September.

Safety rule

Avoid water contact after rain, stay off closed or fenced areas, and check safe-eating advice before keeping fish.

LA River fish

Fish you may hear about in and around the river

State water board materials and local public river sources show a mix of warm-water urban fish here, including carp, tilapia, catfish, sunfish, and occasional bass.

Carp

Strong bottom fish and one of the classic urban-river targets.

Tilapia

Warm-water fish often found in slower LA River reaches.

Green sunfish

Small but aggressive. Happy to bite worms, tiny jigs, and insects.

Black bullhead or other catfish

Bottom fish that tolerate murky city water better than many species.

Largemouth bass

Not everywhere, but absolutely worth chasing in the calmer, cover-filled areas nearby.

Mosquitofish and minnows

Tiny fish that remind you the river is a living system, not just a concrete channel.

Arroyo chub

A native fish that appears in some basin studies and deserves respect.

Steelhead history

Steelhead are part of the river's story, even if modern beginners are not targeting them here.

Best seasons

What to chase through the year

Winter

Stocked trout at city lakes can be the easiest fun. Bass usually slow down in cold water.

Spring

Bass often move shallow. This is a great season for plastic worms and moving baits near cover.

Summer

Catfish, carp, sunfish, and warm-water river fishing shine. Fish early and late to beat the heat.

Fall

Feeding fish can be active again, especially bass and panfish around edges and structure.

Safety first

Rules every kid should know

  • Fish only where the public is allowed to be.
  • Never climb fences, storm channels, or slippery concrete banks.
  • Avoid water contact after rain because runoff can carry bacteria, chemicals, and debris.
  • Wear closed-toe shoes and watch for hooks on the ground.
  • Wash hands after fishing, especially before eating.
  • Check local safe-eating advice before keeping fish from urban water.

Smart food rule

Not every fish should go home for dinner.

OEHHA has water-by-water eating advice for places like Castaic Lake, Castaic Lagoon, Santa Fe Dam Lake, and Legg Lake. Use that advice every time if you plan to eat your catch.
