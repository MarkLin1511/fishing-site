First tackle box

Buy this before you buy twenty random lures.

  • Size 6 bait-holder hooks for worms and panfish
  • 1/0 to 3/0 worm hooks for bass plastics
  • Small split shot weights
  • A few clip-on bobbers
  • 1/8-ounce jig heads
  • Soft plastic worms in green pumpkin or black
  • Inline spinners or tiny spoons for trout and panfish
  • Needle-nose pliers and line cutters

One-line answer

If you buy only one thing, buy a 6'6" medium spinning combo.

That one sentence solves most beginner gear problems. It covers enough fish types to keep learning simple and fun.

Line guide

What line should you use?

8-pound monofilament

The easiest first line. Cheap, simple, and perfect for general beginner use.

10-pound monofilament

A little tougher for catfish, carp, and snaggy banks. Still beginner-friendly.

15-pound braid + 8-pound leader

Good once you know your knots. Better feel, longer casts, more complexity.

Best beginner baits

Use simple baits that catch fish in LA

Nightcrawlers

Top easy bait for bluegill, sunfish, trout, and catfish.

Soft plastic worms

The classic bass bait. Start with green pumpkin or black.

Mini jigs

Great for panfish and crappie around docks, brush, and shoreline cover.

Chicken liver or stink bait

Classic catfish move for evening fishing.

Corn or dough bait

Simple option for carp in lakes and slower river water.

Do buy

Smart first purchases

Rod, line, hooks, weights, bobbers, pliers, soft worms, and one small tackle box.

Do not buy yet

Stuff beginners often waste money on

Huge lure packs, baitcasters, giant tackle backpacks, heavy surf rods, and ten different line types.

Simple wins early.

Your job is to learn fish behavior, not to own every gadget.
