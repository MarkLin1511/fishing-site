Quick start

If you only remember four things, remember these.

1

Pick a simple spinning rod

A 6'6" medium spinning combo is the easiest all-around choice for bass, bluegill, and catfish.

2

Use easy bait first

Worms, mini jigs, and soft plastic worms catch fish without needing expert casting.

3

Fish legal public water

Stay in public access areas, follow posted rules, and check the California rules map before you go.

4

Start near calm banks

Quiet edges, shade lines, weed pockets, and corners are easier for beginners than deep open water.

Cool fish around LA

These are the main freshwater fish beginners should know.

This is a practical "who you will actually hear about and catch" list for Los Angeles freshwater fishing. It is not a full scientific list of every fish ever recorded in the county.

Largemouth bass

The star fish for many LA anglers. Loves weeds, shade, and soft plastic worms.

Find them in: LA River slow pools, Hansen Dam, Santa Fe Dam, Castaic, Legg Lake

Bluegill and other sunfish

Great first fish. Small mouths, fun fights, and happy to eat worms under a bobber.

Find them in: almost every beginner lake around LA

Channel catfish

Whiskered bottom fish that love stinkier bait and often bite best in the evening.

Find them in: Legg Lake, Santa Fe Dam, Castaic Lake

Common carp

Big, smart fish that can pull hard. Corn, dough bait, or simple bottom rigs can work.

Find them in: LA River, Santa Fe Dam, Castaic, Legg Lake

Rainbow trout

Usually a cool-season stocked fish in city waters. Good for winter and early spring trips.

Find them in: stocked local lakes like Legg Lake

Crappie

Schooling fish that like small jigs and minnows. They often hang near structure.

Find them in: some LA-area lakes and reservoirs

Tilapia

Warm-water fish often found in urban waterways. They show up in parts of the LA River system.

Find them in: warmer LA River reaches and nearby waters

Black bullhead and other catfish cousins

Tough little bottom fish that can handle murkier urban water.

Find them in: LA River and some local lakes

Striped bass

The bigger, stronger cousin you chase in larger reservoirs when you want more power.

Find them in: Castaic Lake and other big Southern California reservoirs

Steelhead trout

A historical LA River fish and a reminder that this river once held famous migrators from the ocean.

Think of them as a history lesson, not a beginner target.

First trip checklist

Here is a calm, smart plan for your very first day.

Get legal

If you are 16 or older, buy a California fishing license. If you are 15 or younger, you can fish without one.

Pack light

Bring one rod, small pliers, extra hooks, a bobber, split shot, soft worms, water, sunscreen, and a hat.

Go at the right time

Morning and evening are usually easier than hot midday hours, especially for bass and catfish.

Start with the easy rig

For bluegill, use a bobber and a small worm. For bass, throw a simple plastic worm or jig near cover.

Be patient

Fishing is not a video game. Slow down, watch the line, and move spots if nothing happens after a fair try.

Leave the place cleaner

Pick up line scraps, bait cups, and trash. Rivers and lakes need kind anglers.

Big reminder

You do not need expensive gear to start.

A simple medium spinning combo, 8-pound line, and a few small baits can teach you almost everything you need to know at first.
